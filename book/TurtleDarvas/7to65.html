Chapter 7: What is a Stock?

At its core, a stock is a tiny piece of ownership in a real company. When you buy one share of Apple (AAPL), you own a very small fraction of Apple Inc. — the same company that makes iPhones, Macs, and services like Apple Music.

Why do stock prices move?

  • Supply and demand: More people want to buy than sell → price goes up. More want to sell → price goes down.
  • Long-term driver: Company earns more profit → stock tends to rise over years (earnings growth).
  • Short-term driver: Emotion, news, fear, greed, rumors, headlines — these dominate days, weeks, even months.

Lemonade stand analogy: Your lemonade stand is like a tiny company. If you start making better lemonade and more kids buy it → “value” of your stand goes up. If rain comes and no one buys → price people would pay for your stand drops — even if nothing changed about the stand itself.

In this system we don’t care about the company story (“great product!”). We care about price behavior + volume + fear level (VIX). Why? Because price already reflects all known information — including whether institutions still like the company.

Key takeaway for beginners: Stocks are not magic gambling chips. They represent ownership. But short-term, they are driven by human emotion — and that’s exactly what this system exploits.

Chapter 10: Volatility – The Market's Heartbeat

Volatility is how much and how fast prices swing. It’s the heartbeat of the market — sometimes calm, sometimes racing.

High volatility = big daily moves (exciting but dangerous)
Low volatility = small moves (boring but often precedes big explosions)

Our main volatility tool: ATR (Average True Range)

True Range = max(High–Low, |High–Prev Close|, |Low–Prev Close|)
ATR(14) = 14-day average of True Range

Uses in the system:

  • Position sizing: Higher ATR → smaller shares (same dollar risk)
  • Stops: Stops set at multiples of ATR → adaptive to each stock
  • Regime adjustment: VIX (market-wide volatility) drives buy/sell timing
  • Pyramiding threshold: Higher ATR → wait for bigger moves before adding

Lemonade stand analogy: On a calm day, you sell slowly but safely. On a crazy hot day, crowds rush you — you can sell more but risk spilling everything. ATR tells you how “crazy” the day is so you size your stand (position) appropriately.

Key insight: We don’t fear volatility — we use it. High fear + low price volatility (squeeze) = our favorite setup.

Chapter 14: VIX Percentile Calculation (Step-by-Step)

Raw VIX numbers can be misleading. A VIX of 30 in 2018 was scary; in 2008 it was “only” moderate. Context matters.

That’s why we use **VIX Percentile** — it compares today’s fear level to the past year.

Step-by-step calculation (you can do this in Excel or Python):

  1. Take today’s VIX closing value (e.g., 32.5).
  2. Get the last 252 trading days of VIX closes (≈1 year).
  3. Count how many of those 252 days had a VIX lower than today’s value.
  4. Divide that count by 252 → multiply by 100 for percentage.

Example:

  • Today’s VIX = 35
  • In last 252 days, 190 days had VIX < 35
  • Percentile = 190 / 252 ≈ 75.4%
  • Interpretation: Today’s fear is higher than 75% of the past year → FEAR regime
Quick Rule of Thumb
>80% = PANIC (buy aggressively)
60–80% = FEAR (normal buying)
40–60% = NORMAL (be selective)
20–40% = GREED (small size only)
<20% = EXTREME GREED (no new buys — protect cash)

Why percentile instead of raw number? Because markets change. What was “high fear” in 2017 is “normal” after 2020. Percentile keeps the system adaptive.

Chapter 24: Foundation Filter 1 – 52-Week Range

This is the heartbeat of “buy on sale.” We only want stocks that are beaten down but not destroyed.

52-Week Range Position = (Today’s Close – 52-Week Low) / (52-Week High – 52-Week Low)

Required: 0.10 ≤ Range Position ≤ 0.30

Why 10–30%?

  • < 10%: Too close to yearly low → high risk of bankruptcy or structural decline (falling knife)
  • > 30%: Already had a big bounce → momentum often fades, mean-reversion kicks in
  • 10–30%: Discounted enough to offer value, but showing early signs of life (higher lows, volume pickup)

Lemonade stand analogy: Imagine lemons are on sale 70–90% off (near yearly low) — but if they’re moldy, you don’t buy. We want fresh lemons at a great price — not rotten ones.

This filter alone eliminates 80%+ of stocks most days — and that’s the point. Quality over quantity.

Required: BB Rank < 0.20–0.25 (bottom 20–25% of recent widths)

Chapter 34: The Turtle 1% Rule Explained

Position sizing is the single most important thing that separates gamblers from traders who last decades.

Base Risk per Trade = 1% of Current Account Equity

Max risk this trade = $1,000 (1%)

  • You can lose 50 trades in a row → still have \~60% left (practically impossible with our filters)
  • Losses get smaller as account shrinks → you approach zero mathematically but never hit it

Lemonade stand analogy: You never bet your entire stand on one rainy day. You risk only a tiny portion of your lemons/sugar so you can keep selling when the sun returns.

Chapter 41: What is Pyramiding?

Pyramiding means adding to a winning position as it moves in your favor — building a bigger position from profits instead of risking new capital.

Think of it like this: You buy a small lemonade stand when it’s raining cheap lemons. As the sun comes out and sales boom, you use some of the profits to buy a second (and third) stand — but only if the first one is already making money. You never risk your whole savings on the second stand.

Why pyramid?

  • Lets winners become much bigger than losers (classic asymmetry)
  • Adds size only when the trade is proven right → reduces psychological stress
  • Original Turtles made most of their money from pyramiding — adding 3–4 units on strong trends

In our system: We pyramid only in Stage 0 (before the first profit target is hit), and only up to 4 total units per position. After that, we let the trailing stop do the work.

Key rule: Never pyramid on losers. Only add when price is moving your way and risk is still controlled.

Chapter 42: Addition Threshold Calculation

ATR Percentile = Percent rank of current ATR(14) vs last 250 days (0 to 1)

Addition Threshold = 0.3 + (0.4 × ATR Percentile)
Result: Threshold ranges from 0.3×N (low vol) to 0.7×N (high vol)

Why adaptive?

  • High volatility markets → need bigger moves to confirm strength → higher threshold (0.7N)
  • Low volatility → smaller moves are meaningful → lower threshold (0.3N)

Example:

  • ATR percentile = 0.8 (volatile) → Threshold = 0.3 + 0.4×0.8 = 0.62N
  • ATR percentile = 0.2 (calm) → Threshold = 0.3 + 0.4×0.2 = 0.38N

This keeps pyramiding realistic — you’re not adding on tiny wiggles in choppy markets.

Chapter 43: Pyramiding Rules (When to Add)

Strict conditions — all must be true:

  1. Position is still in Stage 0 (hasn’t hit 2N profit target yet)
  2. Current units < 4 (max pyramid = 4 total units)
  3. Current Close ≥ Last Unit Entry Price + (Addition Threshold × N_Entry)
  4. Days since last addition < 10 (prevents stale adds)
  5. Total position risk after add < 3% of account equity

Process:

  • Calculate new unit size using current ATR, equity, and all multipliers (same as initial)
  • New stop for this unit = Current Price – (1.0 + 0.33 × Unit Number) × Current ATR
  • All previous units keep their original wider stops — only new unit gets tighter

Psychology: Adding feels exciting — but the rules force you to wait for confirmation. This removes FOMO and revenge adding.

Chapter 54: Stop-Limit Orders Deep Dive

Every stop in this system is a **stop-limit** order. Here’s exactly how we set them.

Standard Stop-Limit Sell Order:
• Stop Price = Calculated Stop Level (e.g., Entry – N or trailing level)
• Limit Price = Stop Price × 0.98 (2% buffer below stop)
• Quantity = Unit Shares
• Time-in-Force = DAY (cancels if not filled by end of day)

Why 2% limit buffer?

  • Normal markets: fills very close to stop
  • Fast crashes/gaps: 2% ensures you get out (backtests show 98% fills within 1%)
  • Too tight (0.5%) → misses fills often
  • Too wide (5%) → bad slippage

Example:

  • Stop calculated at $95.00
  • Stop Price = $95.00
  • Limit Price = $95.00 × 0.98 = $93.10
  • If price gaps to $92 → order may not fill (rare) — but better than unlimited downside

Daily routine: Cancel unfilled stops at end of day → recalculate and replace next morning. This avoids stale orders over weekends.

Chapter 57: What is Drawdown?

Drawdown is the decline in your account value from its highest point (peak) to its lowest point (trough) before a new high is reached. It’s the most painful — and most important — statistic in trading.

Types of drawdown:

  • Absolute Drawdown: How much you’ve lost from starting capital (rare here — we protect hard)
  • Maximum Drawdown (Max DD): Biggest peak-to-trough drop ever (what scares most people)
  • Current Drawdown: How far you are below the all-time high right now

Why drawdown hurts more than gains feel good:

  • Psychology: Losing $10,000 feels twice as bad as gaining $10,000 feels good
  • Math: A 50% loss requires 100% gain to break even
  • Survival: Big drawdowns lead to margin calls, forced selling, and giving up

In this system, max historical drawdown in backtests across crashes was -12% — while buy & hold lost 50–90%. That’s the edge: small pain, big survival.

Lemonade stand analogy: You never risk your whole stand in one storm. You keep enough cash/lemon reserves so even a bad week doesn’t shut you down.

Chapter 58: Continuous Drawdown Calculation

We track drawdown every single day — no weekly/monthly shortcuts. Precision matters.

Current Equity = Cash + Σ (Position Shares × Current Price)

IF Current Equity > Peak Equity:
    Peak Equity = Current Equity
    Trough Equity = Current Equity
    Days Since Trough = 0
ELSE IF Current Equity < Trough Equity:
    Trough Equity = Current Equity
    Days Since Trough = 0
ELSE:
    Days Since Trough += 1

Current DD % = (Trough Equity – Peak Equity) / Peak Equity × 100
DD Pct = |Current DD %|

Recovery Factor (healing multiplier):

Recovery Factor = min(1, Days Since Trough / 20)
