1.1 What is Trading?

At its simplest level, trading is buying something at a low price and selling it at a higher price to make a profit. You already do this in everyday life: you buy a video game on sale for $30, and when it becomes rare, you sell it to a collector for $100; you buy a house for $200,000, fix it up, and sell it for $300,000; you buy apples at the farmers market for $1 each and sell them at a roadside stand for $2.

Trading stocks is exactly the same concept‚Äîyou're buying a small piece of a company (a share) hoping to sell it later at a higher price.

1.2 Stocks: What Are You Actually Buying?

A stock (also called a share or equity) represents ownership in a company. If a company is worth $1 million and they divide ownership into 1 million shares, each share is worth $1. If you buy 100 shares, you own 0.01% of that company. When you own a stock, you're entitled to a portion of the company's profits (sometimes paid as dividends), voting rights on major company decisions, and the right to sell your shares to someone else.

1.5 How Stock Prices Move

Stock prices move based on supply and demand. More buyers than sellers = price up. More sellers than buyers = price down. What drives supply and demand? Company performance (earnings, revenue, new products), economic factors (interest rates, employment, inflation), market sentiment (fear, greed, panic, euphoria), institutional activity (large funds buying or selling), and news and events (mergers, scandals, global events).

The Turtle-Darvas system focuses on one primary driver: market sentiment. We buy when sentiment is extremely negative (panic) and sell when sentiment becomes positive (greed).

2.2 Rule 1: Only Buy on Sale

\[0.10 \le \frac{P_{\text{current}} - P_{\text{52L}}}{P_{\text{52H}} - P_{\text{52L}}} \le 0.30\]

In Plain English: Only buy stocks that are 10% to 30% above their lowest price in the last year.

2.2.1 Why This Works

Every stock has a 52-week high (the highest price in the last year) and a 52-week low (the lowest price in the last year). When a stock is near its 52-week low (within 10%), something is usually wrong ‚Äì the company might be facing bankruptcy, losing money, or falling out of favor. These are "value traps" ‚Äì they look cheap but keep getting cheaper. When a stock is more than 30% above its 52-week low, you've missed the best part of the recovery. You're buying after the smart money has already accumulated, and you're at higher risk of buying the top. The 10-30% zone is the "sweet spot" where the worst of the selling is over, institutions have started accumulating, and there's still plenty of upside potential.

Detailed Example: Company XYZ: 52-week high: $100, 52-week low: $50. Current price $55 ‚Üí Range = (55-50)/(100-50)=5/50=0.10 (10%) ‚Äì at lower limit, acceptable. Three months later at $65 ‚Üí Range = 15/50=0.30 (30%) ‚Äì at upper limit, still acceptable. Six months later at $85 ‚Üí Range = 35/50=0.70 (70%) ‚Äì too high, wait for next pullback.

3.2 The Buy High, Sell Low Phenomenon

Why do intelligent people do the opposite of what they should? Because they let emotions drive decisions: terror at bottoms makes them sell, greed at tops makes them buy. The Turtle-Darvas system replaces emotion with rules.

3.3 The Psychology of Loss Aversion

Nobel Prize-winning research shows that losing $100 hurts about twice as much as gaining $100 feels good. This 2:1 ratio causes investors to hold losing positions hoping to "get even" (avoid realizing the loss) and sell winning positions too early to "lock in" the gain.

The Math of the Disposition Effect: If you cut losses at 10% and let winners run to 50%, your winners can absorb multiple losses: 1 win at +50% = 5 losses at -10% (break-even). The Turtle-Darvas system's trailing stops ensure you cut losses quickly and let winners run.

Chapter 8: Understanding Price Charts

A price chart is a picture of how buyers and sellers have fought. Candlesticks show open, high, low, close. Green means buyers won the day; red means sellers won. We look for higher highs/higher lows (uptrend), sideways consolidation (energy building), and panic spikes (capitulation).
