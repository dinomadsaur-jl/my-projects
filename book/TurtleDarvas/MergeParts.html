1.1 What is Trading?

At its simplest level, trading is buying something at a low price and selling it at a higher price to make a profit. You already do this in everyday life:

  • You buy a video game on sale for $30, and when it becomes rare, you sell it to a collector for $100.
  • You buy a house for $200,000, fix it up, and sell it for $300,000.
  • You buy apples at the farmers market for $1 each and sell them at a roadside stand for $2.

Trading stocks is exactly the same concept‚Äîyou're buying a small piece of a company (a share) hoping to sell it later at a higher price.

1.2 Stocks: What Are You Actually Buying?

A stock (also called a share or equity) represents ownership in a company. If a company is worth $1 million and they divide ownership into 1 million shares, each share is worth $1. If you buy 100 shares, you own 0.01% of that company.

When you own a stock, you're entitled to:

  • A portion of the company's profits (sometimes paid as dividends)
  • Voting rights on major company decisions
  • The right to sell your shares to someone else

  • People hold losing positions hoping to "get even" (avoid realizing the loss)

If you cut losses at 10% and let winners run to 50%, your winners can absorb multiple losses:

3.7 Recency Bias and Anchoring

6.1 Who Was Nicolas Darvas?

Nicolas Darvas was a Hungarian dancer who, along with his partner Julia, became one of the highest-paid dance teams in the world in the 1950s. While touring the globe ‚Äì performing in London, Paris, Tokyo, and New York ‚Äì he became fascinated with the stock market.

Between 1952 and 1959, he turned $36,000 into over $2 million ‚Äì an astronomical sum at the time. He wrote a book about his experience, "How I Made $2,000,000 in the Stock Market," which became a classic and is still in print today.

6.2 The Darvas Box Theory: Core Concept

Darvas noticed that stocks often trade in "boxes" ‚Äì ranges between support and resistance. A stock would trade between, say, $50 and $55 for weeks, then break out to a new box ($55-$60), then another, and another.

The key insight: Buy when the stock breaks out of a box to a new high. Sell when it breaks down below the box.

Darvas Box Example:
  • Stock trades between $50 and $55 for 3 weeks ‚Äì this is Box 1
  • Stock breaks above $55 on high volume ‚Äì BUY signal
  • Stock now trades between $55 and $60 ‚Äì this is Box 2
  • If stock falls below $55, SELL (stop loss at box bottom)
  • If stock breaks above $60, add to position (new box)

6.3 How Darvas Identified Boxes

Darvas drew his boxes manually on price charts. He would look for:

  • A clear top and bottom: Price bounced between these levels multiple times
  • Duration: Boxes typically lasted 1-4 weeks
  • Volume: Volume would dry up inside the box, then surge on breakout
  • Consolidation: The box represented a period of consolidation before the next move

6.4 The Darvas Entry Method

Darvas only bought stocks making new highs. This was counterintuitive ‚Äì most investors then (and now) prefer to buy low and sell high. Darvas bought high and sold higher.

Entry criteria:

  1. Stock must be in a strong uptrend (making higher highs and higher lows)
  2. Stock must form a box (consolidation) for at least 1 week
  3. Stock must break above the box on high volume
  4. Buy on the breakout

  • It broke above $15 on huge volume ‚Äì Darvas bought at $15.25

  • Continued up to $25 before breaking down
  • Darvas sold at $23.50, making over 50% in a few months

6.5 The Darvas Exit Method

Darvas used a simple stop loss: the bottom of the current box. As long as the stock stayed above the box bottom, he held. If it broke below, he sold immediately.

This is one of the earliest forms of trailing stops. As the stock moved up to new boxes, the stop moved up with it.

Darvas Stop Rule:

Stop = Bottom of Current Box

If stock breaks above to new box, new stop = bottom of new box

Darvas's method worked because it exploited human psychology:

  • Institutions buy breakouts: Big money waits for confirmation before buying

Chapter 7: What is a Stock? (Complete Beginner's Guide)

7.1 What is a Stock?

A stock represents ownership in a company. When you buy a share of stock, you're buying a tiny piece of that company. You become a part-owner, entitled to a portion of its profits and assets.

Simple analogy: Imagine a pizza worth $100. If you cut it into 100 slices, each slice is worth $1. If you buy 10 slices, you own 10% of the pizza. If the pizza's value increases to $200, your slices are now worth $2 each. If the pizza burns and becomes worthless, your slices are worthless too.

Companies are the same. They divide ownership into shares. If you own shares, you own a piece of the company. If the company becomes more valuable, your shares become more valuable. If the company fails, your shares become worthless.

7.2 Why Do Companies Issue Stock?

Companies issue stock to raise money. This is called "going public" through an Initial Public Offering (IPO). When a company goes public, it sells shares to investors and uses the money to:

  • Expand operations
  • Build new factories
  • Hire more employees
  • Pay off debt
  • Fund research and development
  • Acquire other companies

After the IPO, shares trade on stock exchanges like the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) or NASDAQ. Investors buy and sell shares among themselves. The company doesn't get any money from these secondary trades ‚Äì the money goes between buyers and sellers.

7.3 Types of Stock

7.3.1 Common Stock

This is what most people mean when they say "stock." Common stockholders have:

  • Voting rights: You can vote on company matters (one share = one vote)
  • Dividend rights: You may receive dividends if the company pays them
  • Residual claim: If the company goes bankrupt, you get what's left after all debts are paid (usually nothing)

7.3.2 Preferred Stock

Preferred stock is a hybrid between common stock and bonds. Preferred stockholders:

  • No voting rights: You can't vote on company matters
  • Fixed dividends: You receive a fixed dividend, usually higher than common stock
  • Priority: If the company goes bankrupt, preferred stockholders get paid before common stockholders

The Turtle-Darvas system only trades common stock. Preferred stock doesn't have the same volatility patterns we need.

8.4 Key Chart Concepts

8.4.1 Trend

Trend is the direction of price movement over time.

  • Uptrend: Higher highs and higher lows. Buyers in control.
  • Downtrend: Lower highs and lower lows. Sellers in control.
  • Sideways (range): No clear direction. Consolidation.

The Turtle-Darvas system only buys stocks in uptrends. We identify uptrends by:

  • Price above 200-day moving average
  • Higher highs and higher lows
  • Breakouts to new highs

8.4.2 Support and Resistance

Support: A price level where buying pressure is strong enough to stop a downtrend. Think of it as a floor.

Resistance: A price level where selling pressure is strong enough to stop an uptrend. Think of it as a ceiling.

When support or resistance breaks, it often signals a significant move:

  • Break above resistance = bullish signal (our breakouts)
  • Break below support = bearish signal

8.4.3 Volume

Volume bars at the bottom of the chart show how many shares traded each day. Volume confirms price moves:

  • Up on high volume = strong buying, likely to continue
  • Up on low volume = weak buying, may reverse
  • Down on high volume = strong selling, likely to continue
  • Down on low volume = weak selling, may reverse

Our system requires volume confirmation on breakouts (Volume Z-Score > 1.5).

8.5 Chart Patterns Every Trader Should Know

8.5.1 Bullish Patterns

Higher Highs and Higher Lows: The classic uptrend pattern. Each peak is higher than the last, each trough is higher than the last.

Cup and Handle: Price forms a U-shaped cup (decline then recovery), then a small consolidation (handle), then breaks out. Very reliable bullish pattern.

Bull Flag: A sharp move up (flagpole), then a small pullback in a channel (flag), then continuation up. Shows buying pressure.

Double Bottom: Price hits a low, bounces, then returns to the same low and bounces again. Forms a "W" shape. Strong reversal signal.

8.5.2 Bearish Patterns

Lower Highs and Lower Lows: The classic downtrend pattern.

Head and Shoulders: Three peaks: middle (head) higher than two shoulders. When price breaks below the "neckline," it's a strong sell signal.

Bear Flag: Sharp move down, small pullback, then continuation down.

Double Top: Price hits a high, pulls back, then returns to same high and fails to break through. Forms an "M" shape.

The Turtle-Darvas system focuses on bullish patterns for entries (breakouts) and doesn't use bearish patterns (we don't short).

8.6 How to Read a Chart ‚Äì Step by Step

Step 1: Determine the timeframe. Start with daily charts. If you need more detail, go to 4-hour. If you need context, check weekly.

Step 2: Identify the trend. Look at the big picture. Is the stock making higher highs and higher lows? Is it above its 200-day moving average? This tells you whether to consider buying.

Step 3: Identify key support and resistance. Where has the stock reversed in the past? These are your potential breakout levels.

Step 4: Look at recent price action. Is the stock consolidating (tight range)? This is what we want ‚Äì a squeeze before a breakout.

Step 5: Check volume. Is volume confirming the price action? High volume on up days, low volume on down days is bullish.

Step 6: Apply your indicators. Run your ATR, Bollinger Bands, OBV, etc. Are they confirming what you see?

Step 7: Make a decision. Based on all the above, is this a buy, hold, or sell?

  • Ignoring volume: Breakouts without volume often fail.
  • Changing timeframes to fit a bias: If it doesn't look good on daily, it's not a trade.
  • Not using stop losses: Charts can't predict everything. Always have a stop.

Chapter 9: Volume ‚Äì The Fuel of Markets

üìò FOR BEGINNERS:

Volume is the number of shares traded. It's like the fuel that moves markets. Without volume, price moves are weak and unreliable. This chapter explains everything you need to know about volume and how we use it in the Turtle-Darvas system.

9.1 What is Volume?

Volume is the total number of shares traded during a given period. For daily charts, it's the total shares traded that day. Volume is shown as bars at the bottom of the chart.

Simple analogy: Think of price as the car and volume as the gas. A car can move without gas (coasting downhill), but it won't go far. Sustainable moves need gas (volume).

9.2 Why Volume Matters

Volume tells you the strength behind a price move:

  • High volume + price up = Strong buying pressure. Many shares changing hands, many buyers. This move is likely to continue.
  • Low volume + price up = Weak buying pressure. Few shares trading, few buyers. This move may reverse.
  • High volume + price down = Strong selling pressure. Many sellers. Move likely to continue.
  • Low volume + price down = Weak selling pressure. Few sellers. Move may reverse.

Volume also tells you about participation:

  • High volume = Institutions are participating. Big money is moving.
  • Low volume = Mostly retail traders. Moves are less reliable.

9.3 Volume Patterns

9.3.1 Volume on Breakouts

Breakouts on high volume are much more reliable than breakouts on low volume. When a stock breaks above resistance on high volume, it shows that institutions are buying. When it breaks on low volume, it's often a "false breakout" that will fail.

  • Selling climax: Extreme volume on a down day ‚Äì panic selling, often marks a bottom
  • Buying climax: Extreme volume on an up day ‚Äì euphoria, often marks a top

The Turtle-Darvas system buys after selling climaxes when volume returns to normal and price stabilizes.

9.3.3 Volume Dry Up

When volume dries up to very low levels, it often means a move is ending or a consolidation is beginning. Our Bollinger squeeze condition often coincides with low volume ‚Äì the market is waiting for a catalyst.

Chapter 10: Volatility ‚Äì The Market's Heartbeat

üìò FOR BEGINNERS:

Volatility measures how much and how quickly prices move. It's the market's heartbeat ‚Äì sometimes calm and steady, sometimes racing with fear. Understanding volatility is essential because the Turtle-Darvas system uses it for position sizing, stops, and entries.

10.1 What is Volatility?

Volatility is the degree of variation in price over time. A highly volatile stock moves a lot (up or down) in a short period. A low volatility stock moves slowly and steadily.

Simple analogy: Think of two dogs:

  • A Labrador retriever (low volatility) ‚Äì calm, steady, predictable
  • A Jack Russell terrier (high volatility) ‚Äì bouncing off walls, unpredictable

Stocks are the same. Utilities (like Duke Energy) are Labradors ‚Äì slow and steady. Tech stocks (like Tesla) are Jack Russells ‚Äì bouncing all over the place.

10.2 Why Volatility Matters

Volatility affects every aspect of trading:

  • Position sizing: Higher volatility = smaller positions (same dollar risk)
  • Stop placement: Higher volatility = wider stops (to avoid being shaken out)
  • Entry timing: Higher volatility = more frequent opportunities
  • Exit timing: Higher volatility = more false breakouts
  • Risk: Higher volatility = higher potential returns AND higher potential losses

The Turtle-Darvas system normalizes everything by volatility. We don't think in dollars ‚Äì we think in "N" (ATR units). This makes the system work across all stocks.

10.3 Measuring Volatility: ATR (Average True Range)

The most common volatility measure is ATR (Average True Range), developed by J. Welles Wilder in his 1978 book "New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems."

10.3.1 True Range Calculation

True Range measures the true daily range, accounting for gaps:

True Range = max of:
  • Current High - Current Low
  • |Current High - Previous Close|
  • |Current Low - Previous Close|

This captures the entire day's movement, even if there's a gap from the previous close.

10.3.2 ATR Calculation

ATR is simply the moving average of True Range, typically over 14 days:

ATR(14) = Average of True Range over last 14 days

ATR tells you how much a stock typically moves each day. If ATR is $5, the stock typically moves $5 per day (up or down).

11.2 Moving Averages

11.2.1 Simple Moving Average (SMA)

SMA is the average price over a specified period. It smooths out price data to show the trend.

SMA(20) = Sum of last 20 closes / 20

How we use it:

  • 200-day SMA: Long-term trend filter (only buy above 200-day)
  • 20-day SMA: Used in Bollinger Bands
  • 10-day EMA: Used in panic entries (price must be above 10-day EMA)

11.2.2 Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

EMA gives more weight to recent prices. It reacts faster to new information.

EMA = (Close - Previous EMA) √ó Multiplier + Previous EMA

Multiplier = 2 / (Period + 1)

How we use it: 10-day EMA for panic entries ‚Äì price must be above EMA, showing short-term momentum.

11.4 Bollinger Bands

Developed by John Bollinger, Bollinger Bands consist of three lines:

  • Middle band: 20-day SMA
  • Upper band: Middle + 2 √ó 20-day standard deviation
  • Lower band: Middle - 2 √ó 20-day standard deviation
Bollinger Bands:

Middle = SMA(20)

Upper = Middle + 2 √ó STDEV(20)

Lower = Middle - 2 √ó STDEV(20)

Width = (Upper - Lower) / Middle

How we use it:

  • Bollinger Squeeze: When width is in bottom 20% of its 60-day range, volatility is low ‚Äì potential breakout coming
  • We require BB Rank < 0.20 as a foundation filter

11.5 OBV (On-Balance Volume)

OBV adds volume on up days and subtracts on down days. It shows whether volume is flowing into or out of a stock.

OBV Calculation:

If Close > Previous Close: OBV = Previous OBV + Volume

If Close < Previous Close: OBV = Previous OBV - Volume

If Close = Previous Close: OBV = Previous OBV

How we use it:

  • OBV slope > 0 (rising) shows accumulation
  • We require this as a foundation filter
  • Divergence between OBV and price can signal reversals

11.6 MFI (Money Flow Index)

MFI is like RSI but volume-weighted. It measures buying and selling pressure.

MFI Calculation:
  1. Typical Price = (High + Low + Close) / 3
  2. Raw Money Flow = Typical Price √ó Volume
  3. Positive Flow = sum of Raw Money Flow on up days
  4. Negative Flow = sum of Raw Money Flow on down days
  5. Money Ratio = Positive Flow / Negative Flow
  6. MFI = 100 - 100 / (1 + Money Ratio)

How we use it:

  • PANIC regime: MFI < 25 (oversold) and rising
  • NORMAL regime: MFI between 30-70
  • GREED regime: MFI < 60 (not overbought)

11.7 Volume Z-Score

Z-score standardizes volume so we can compare across stocks. It tells us how unusual current volume is.

Volume Z-Score:

Z = (Current Volume - Average Volume(20)) / Standard Deviation(20)

How we use it:

  • Z > 1.5 = significant volume surge (required for entries)
  • Z > 2.0 = extreme volume surge (used in GREED regime)
